About

Academic Profile

My work sits at the intersection of strategic management, entrepreneurship, and institutional analysis. I study how firms in informal and fragmented markets create growth, structure competition, reproduce commercial capabilities, and sustain market ecosystems through relational, social, and locally legitimate practices.

A central thread in my research is that market development in many African settings cannot be fully explained through formal-firm assumptions alone. Instead, I examine how apprenticeship systems, embedded norms, community enforcement, and social coordination shape enterprise creation, market expansion, and long-term resilience.

I am particularly interested in generating theory from under-studied market contexts and combining qualitative, experimental, and quasi-experimental approaches to explain how informal institutions influence strategic action, organizational reproduction, and economic outcomes.

Research

Research Themes

Competition-Building in Informal Markets

Investigating how incumbents deliberately create, support, and regulate new competitors in fragmented markets, challenging conventional zero-sum assumptions about rivalry and strategic exclusion.

Growth and Scaling in Under-Institutionalized Markets

Examining how firms expand through social coordination, indigenous systems, and non-bureaucratic mechanisms in environments where formal market-supporting institutions remain weak or uneven.

Embedded Fragility in Carbon Markets

Exploring how behavioral learning, regulatory dependence, and diffusion dynamics interact to generate vulnerability in emerging carbon-market systems.

Experimental and Causal Evidence in Informal Economies

Using field-based causal and quasi-experimental approaches to identify how institutional arrangements influence trust, cooperation, business creation, and market outcomes.

Informal Institutions and Market Organization

Studying how norms, apprenticeship structures, social obligations, and local governance systems organize exchange, regulate entry, and stabilize economic activity over time.

African Markets and Indigenous Organizational Forms

Developing contextually grounded theory from African market systems to broaden strategy and organization scholarship beyond assumptions derived mainly from formal Western firms.

Working Papers

Current Research Pipeline

Manuscript Submission Stage

Beyond the Zero-Sum Game: Deliberate Competition in Fragmented Markets

Abdullahi Olanrewaju Babatunde, Dirk Micheal Boehe

This paper examines how market actors deliberately create and sustain competitors in fragmented informal markets, advancing a perspective that treats competitor creation as an organizing and developmental mechanism rather than a strategic failure.

Manuscript Development Stage

Invisible Growth: How Firms Expand through Informal Institutions in African Markets

Abdullahi Olanrewaju Babatunde, Tim Weiss

This study explores how firms grow in under-institutionalized environments through apprenticeship, relational support, and socially embedded systems of coordination that are often overlooked in mainstream growth theory.

Research Design and Data Collection Stage

Market Practices and Social Impact in Informal Trade Clusters: A Quasi-Experimental Study

Abdullahi Olanrewaju Babatunde, Dirk Micheal Boehe

This project investigates the social and economic consequences of market practices in informal trade clusters using a quasi-experimental design to generate causal evidence on trust, cooperation, and enterprise outcomes.

Research Design Stage

Embedded Fragility: Behavioral and Institutional Vulnerabilities in Emerging Carbon Markets

Abdullahi Olanrewaju Babatunde

This paper develops the concept of embedded fragility to explain how regulatory dependence and socially diffused learning may combine to produce instability in emerging carbon markets.
